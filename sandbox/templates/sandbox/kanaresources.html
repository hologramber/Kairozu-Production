Hiragana & Katakana

The modern Japanese writing system is a combination of kanji (adopted Chinese characters), hiragana, and katakana. Hiragana and katakana are syllabic Japanese characters; each sound in the Japanese language is represented by characters in each system. Because the kana characters do not represent single consonants (except in the case of "n"), the kana are referred to as syllabaries and not alphabets. While kanji are used to write most content words (nouns, stems of verbs and adjectives, personal names, etc) of native Japanese origin, Kairozu sticks to the phonetic spelling of these words (using hiragana) in Chapters 1-8.

Hiragana is used to write the inflected verb and adjective endings which follow a kanji root, various grammatical and function words including particles, as well as miscellaneous other native words for which there are no kanji or whose kanji form is obscure or too formal for the writing purpose. Hiragana can also be used for the phonetic representation of words usually written with kanji.

Katakana is most often used for the transcription of foreign words and names into Japanese, for example "radio" -- a foreign word -- would be written as ラジオ in katakana, not らじお in hiragana. Katakana is also used for onomatopoeia, to indicate emphasis (similar to italics), and for technical and scientific terms.

Romaji is the application of roman characters to writing Japanese words, for example, the word for dog in Japanese is 犬 in kanji, いぬ in hiragana, and "inu" in romaji.
